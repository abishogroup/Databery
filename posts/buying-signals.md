Every vendor now sells "intent data," and every sales team is drowning in alerts. The problem isn't a shortage of signals — it's that most of them don't mean anything. A spike in anonymous web traffic or a generic keyword surge tells you almost nothing about whether a specific account is ready to buy.

After researching thousands of accounts, we've found that a small set of signals reliably precede a real purchase decision. Here are the seven we weight most heavily — and why.

## 1. A new leader in the buying seat

The single strongest signal there is. A new VP, director, or C-level hire in the function you sell to almost always triggers a review of tools, vendors, and priorities. New leaders are hired to change things, and they have a 90-day window to prove it.

## 2. Hiring surges in the relevant team

When an account posts a wave of roles in a particular function, it's funding a priority. Ten new sales hires means a revenue push; a cluster of data engineers means a platform investment. Hiring is budget made visible.

## 3. Funding, M&A, or a reorg

Fresh capital and structural change both reshuffle priorities and unlock spend. The months after a raise or an acquisition are when previously-shelved projects suddenly get green-lit.

## 4. A visible tech or ERP migration

System changes create windows. A company moving off a legacy platform is re-evaluating everything that touches it — which is exactly when an adjacent solution can get a fair hearing.

> The best signals aren't louder — they're more specific. "This person, in this role, just inherited this problem" beats "traffic is up 12%" every time.

## 5. Public commitments and initiatives

When an exec announces a goal — entering a market, hitting a compliance deadline, launching a product — they've created a problem they now have to solve. Map your offer to that commitment and your outreach writes itself.

## 6. Competitor or peer adoption

Few buyers want to be first, and even fewer want to be last. When a direct competitor adopts a category, it raises the cost of inaction for everyone else in the space.

## 7. Repeated, role-relevant engagement

Not one anonymous visit — a pattern. The same account, the right personas, returning to high-intent pages over a short window. On its own it's weak; stacked with any signal above, it's a green light.

## The point: stack signals, don't chase them

No single trigger should send a rep running. The accounts worth prioritising are the ones where two or three of these line up at once — a new leader **and** a hiring surge **and** repeated engagement. That's the combination we score, and it's what turns a target list into a priority order.

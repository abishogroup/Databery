Most teams trying to do account-based selling reach for a data tool first. It makes sense — you need contacts, firmographics, and some kind of intent feed. But a few quarters in, the same complaint surfaces: "We have all this data and the reps still aren't using it." That gap is the difference between data and research.

## Data answers "what." Research answers "so what."

A data subscription is very good at giving you fields: headcount, tech stack, funding, a list of titles. What it can't do is tell you what any of it *means* for your specific deal. A rep doesn't need 40 data points about an account — they need the one sentence that explains why this account, why now, and what to lead with.

> Data is an input. Intelligence is a decision. The job of research is to close the distance between them.

## Why the gap shows up on your win-rate

When the interpretation is left to the rep, three things happen. The best reps spend selling time doing research. The average reps skip it and pitch generically. And the signals that actually matter get buried under the ones that don't. None of that helps your number.

Research flips it. When an account arrives already interpreted — scored, with the relevant signals surfaced and an angle written — every rep starts the conversation from the same strong position.

## What "research" actually adds

- **Judgement** — a human decides which signals matter for your offer, and which are noise.
- **Synthesis** — scattered facts become a short, usable narrative about the account.
- **An angle** — not just who to contact, but why they'd care and how to open.
- **Verification** — claims are checked, so reps can quote them with confidence.

## You probably need both

This isn't an argument to cancel your data tools. They're a useful raw input. The mistake is expecting a database to do a researcher's job. The teams that win pair good data with someone — or some partner — who turns it into a point of view the sales floor can act on.
